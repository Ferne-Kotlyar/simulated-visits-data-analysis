# Aviary Experiment README

Data were collected in February & March of 2017 to 2019.

## Background Information

After the [Betts et al., 2015](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1419522112) paper that described pollinator recognition (whereby *Heliconia tortuosa* reproduces significantly more often after being visited by specialist hummingbirds compared to hand pollination or generalist hummingbirds) came out, we wanted to see if this mechanism applied to other *Heliconia* plants. However, in 2017, we failed to reproduce their suggest mechanism of nectar extraction in *Heliconia tortuosa* itself. Therefore, here, we test whether we can reproduce the pollinator recognition phenomenon (in aviaries_data_analysis.R) and the nectar extraction mechanism (in nectar_removal.R).

## Brief Methods

We followed the same methods as described in [Betts et al., 2015](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1419522112).

## Treatments

#### **Aviary Experiment**

- **HP: Hand Pollination.** In this treatment, we applied outcrossed pollen directly to the stigma of *Heliconia tortuosa* flowers after cleaning from self-pollen.
- **GREH: Green Hermit Hummingbirds (*Phaethornis guy*).** Green Hermits were caught with mist nets, their bills were cleaned from previous pollen, then released into an aviary with *Heliconia tortuosa* flowers and monitored until they fed from the flowers.
- **RTAH: Rufous-tailed Hummingbirds (*Amazilia tzacatl*).** Rufous-tailed hummingbirds were caught with mist nets, their bills were cleaned from previous pollen, then released into an aviary with *Heliconia tortuosa* flowers and monitored until they fed from the flowers.

#### **Nectar Extraction Experiment**

- **HP: Hand Pollination.** Same as above.
- **HPNE: Hand Pollination Nectar Extraction.** Same as above, but the nectar was removed from the nectar chamber using a syringe and oral suction. Here, we were careful not to touch the stigma with the syringe.
